Why Certification Matters

Not All Credentials Are Created Equal

For patients facing skin cancer surgery, knowing your surgeon holds a rigorous, standardized certification isn't a luxury — it's essential. Board certification in Micrographic Dermatologic Surgery (MDS) is that standard.

🏛️

The American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS)

The ABMS is the gold-standard umbrella organization overseeing physician certification in the United States. Member boards — including the American Board of Dermatology — must meet strict criteria for how they develop, administer, and maintain their exams. ABMS certification is the recognized benchmark of physician competency nationwide.

🎓

The American Board of Dermatology (ABD)

The ABD has set the standard for dermatologic competency for over a century. Its subspecialty certification in Micrographic Dermatologic Surgery applies that same rigorous framework to Mohs surgery — ensuring that surgeons who earn this credential have demonstrated mastery of both the surgical and pathological demands of the procedure.

📋

From Varied Pathways to One Unified Standard

Surgeons come to Mohs through different routes — dermatology residency training, accredited fellowships, and other rigorous clinical experiences. What matters now is not how that experience was acquired, but whether it has been validated. The ABD board exam is the single, standardized credential that certifies surgical competency regardless of training background.

📜

A Field Unified Under One Credential

For years, the landscape of Mohs surgery credentials was a patchwork — a non-standardized mix of preceptorships, fellowships, society affiliations, and self-designated titles, each carrying different implications and no uniform standard of evaluation. Patients and referring physicians had no reliable way to compare qualifications. That era has given way to something far more meaningful: a rigorous, comprehensive board examination administered by the American Board of Dermatology and recognized by the ABMS. Fellowships and residency training remain valuable pathways for acquiring the clinical experience needed to perform Mohs surgery — but it is this board certification that now stands as the true, verifiable credential of competency. For the first time, there is a single, objective, nationally recognized answer to the question: is this surgeon qualified?

What's at Stake

The Risks of Choosing an Uncertified Surgeon

Skin cancer surgery on the face, scalp, and hands demands precision. Without verified, standardized training, patients and physicians both bear unnecessary risk.

⚠️ Risks to Patients

  • Incomplete tumor removal due to substandard margin evaluation
  • Higher rates of cancer recurrence requiring additional surgery
  • Poor cosmetic and functional outcomes from inadequate reconstruction
  • No objective assurance that a surgeon's skills have been independently verified
  • Difficulty distinguishing truly qualified Mohs surgeons from those with variable training

⚖️ Risks to Physicians

  • Increased medicolegal exposure without a recognized credential to demonstrate competency
  • Credentialing challenges at hospitals and surgery centers that require verified subspecialty certification
  • Growing competitive disadvantage as board certification becomes the expected standard
  • Reputational risk as patients become more aware of and seek out certified surgeons

A Brief History

Frederic E. Mohs, MD (1910–2002) first performed his namesake procedure at the University of Wisconsin in 1936. Over the following decades, the technique evolved from informal mentorship and preceptorships to structured fellowships and residency training — each a valid path to surgical proficiency. Today, that journey culminates in a single, objective measure: ABD board certification in Micrographic Dermatologic Surgery, the first standardized national credential for Mohs surgeons.

Common Questions

What Patients Ask

What is Mohs micrographic surgery?

Mohs micrographic surgery is a precise surgical technique for removing skin cancer — most commonly basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma — in thin layers. After each layer is removed, the surgeon immediately examines it under a microscope to check for remaining cancer cells. This process continues until all margins are clear, maximizing the cure rate while preserving as much healthy tissue as possible.

What does ABD board certification in MDS mean?

The American Board of Dermatology (ABD) now offers a subspecialty certification in Micrographic Dermatologic Surgery (MDS) — the formal name for Mohs surgery practice at its full scope. To earn this credential, a dermatologist must pass a rigorous, comprehensive examination covering surgical technique, dermatopathology, and reconstructive surgery. This certification is recognized by the American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS), the gold-standard oversight body for physician certification in the United States.

How do I verify my doctor's board certification?

Visit CertificationMatters.org, the free public verification tool maintained by the ABMS. Search for your physician by name to confirm they hold board certification — including in Micrographic Dermatologic Surgery specifically.

Should I ask my surgeon about their certification before scheduling?

Absolutely. Asking whether your surgeon is board certified in Micrographic Dermatologic Surgery by the ABD is a reasonable, appropriate question — just as you would ask a cardiac surgeon or orthopedic specialist about their credentials. A qualified, board-certified surgeon will be glad to confirm this.

Why does standardized certification matter for this procedure specifically?

Mohs surgery is unique in that the operating surgeon must also function as the pathologist, reading their own tissue sections in real time under a microscope. This dual demand — surgical precision plus pathologic expertise — makes rigorous, standardized certification especially important. Board certification ensures that a surgeon's competency in both skill sets has been independently verified.
